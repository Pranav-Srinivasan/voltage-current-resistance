# Voltage, Current & Resistance — Series and Parallel Circuits

## Target
- Grade 8 Cambridge student, zero prior knowledge of electricity
- Build deep foundational intuition so Grade 10 physics feels natural

## Teaching Philosophy
- **No analogies.** Electricity is its own system with its own rules. Teach it from scratch.
- Analogies (water, trucks, factories) break down and confuse students long-term. We avoid them entirely.
- Instead: state each rule plainly, then reinforce it from **multiple angles** — different scenarios, different questions, different ways of seeing the same truth.
- Connect to physics they already know where possible (force, energy, work done) — but as genuine physics connections, not metaphors.
- Every concept is introduced as: "Here is how electrical circuits behave. This is a fact. Let's see it from several angles."
- One idea at a time. Nail it. Then move on.

## Key Physics Connection (not analogy — real physics)
- Students know: **Work = Force x Distance** (W = F x d)
- In electricity: **Work = Charge x Voltage** (W = Q x V)
- Voltage is the electrical equivalent of "force" in energy terms — it's the energy given per unit charge
- This is why voltage is also called **electromotive force (EMF)** — it's the "force" that moves charges and does work
- This connection is used to ground voltage in something real, not as a metaphor

## Section Structure

### Section 1: The Electric Circuit — A Complete Loop

**Core idea:** For electricity to work, charges need a complete path to travel through. No complete path, no current.

**Content:**
- Everything is made of atoms. Atoms have electrons (negative charge) that can move through conductors like metals.
- A **battery** has two terminals: positive (+) and negative (-). Inside the battery, a chemical reaction creates a difference in energy between the two terminals. This energy difference is what pushes electrons through the circuit.
- For electrons to flow, they need a **complete loop** — from one terminal of the battery, through the components, and back to the other terminal.
- If the loop is broken (open switch, broken wire), electrons cannot flow. But the battery still has its energy difference — it's ready to push the moment the loop is completed.
- **Components** are things we put in the path: lamps (convert electrical energy to light), buzzers (to sound), resistors (oppose current).
- **Switches** break or complete the loop.
- Circuit symbols: battery, lamp, switch, resistor (fixed and variable), ammeter, voltmeter.

**Multiple angles:**
- Angle 1: Draw a simple circuit. Trace the path from battery → wire → lamp → wire → battery. Is it a complete loop? Yes. Electrons flow.
- Angle 2: Now open the switch. Is the loop complete? No. No electron flow. But the battery hasn't changed — it's still ready.
- Angle 3: What if a bulb breaks (filament snaps)? The loop is broken at the bulb. Same as opening a switch — no current anywhere.

**Key insight:** The battery provides energy. The complete loop lets electrons carry that energy to the components. Break the loop anywhere, and everything stops.

**Questions:**
- MCQs: What happens when a switch is opened? What does a battery provide? Which of these is a complete circuit (diagram)?
- Subjective: A student connects a battery to a lamp but forgets one wire. Will the lamp light? Explain why. In your own words, why must a circuit be a complete loop?

### Section 2: Current — The Flow of Charge

**Core idea:** Current is simply how much charge flows past a point each second. It is measured, it is real, and it is **never used up**.

**Content:**
- When the circuit is complete, electrons flow through the wires and components. This flow of charge is called **current**.
- Current is measured in **amperes (A)** using an instrument called an **ammeter**.
- An ammeter must be placed **in the path** of the current (in series) — because it needs to count the charge flowing through it.
- Current tells you: how much charge is passing a point every second.

**The most important rule of current — it is NEVER used up:**
- Place an ammeter before a lamp. It reads 0.5 A.
- Place another ammeter after the same lamp. It also reads 0.5 A.
- The same amount of charge that enters the lamp also exits the lamp. No charge is created or destroyed.
- The lamp glows not because it "consumes" electrons, but because electrons transfer their energy to the lamp as they pass through. The electrons themselves keep moving.
- **Rule: In a single loop (series circuit), the current is the same everywhere.**

**Multiple angles:**
- Angle 1 (measurement): Put ammeters at three different points in a series circuit. All read the same value. This is a measurable, verifiable fact.
- Angle 2 (conservation): Charge is conserved. Electrons don't vanish inside a lamp. What goes in must come out. If 100 electrons enter per second, 100 electrons exit per second.
- Angle 3 (energy vs charge): The battery gives energy TO the electrons. The lamp takes energy FROM the electrons. But the electrons themselves keep circulating. The battery eventually runs out of energy to give — that's when it "dies." It never runs out of electrons.
- Angle 4 (common mistake): "The first bulb gets more current than the second." No. Measure it. Same current everywhere. The electrons don't "slow down" or "get used up."

**Key insight:** Current is conserved. Always. In any single path, the current entering equals the current leaving. Electrons carry energy — they deliver it at components — but the electrons themselves keep flowing.

**Questions:**
- MCQs: Where should an ammeter be placed? If ammeter A1 reads 0.3A in a series circuit, what does A2 read? What does current measure?
- Subjective: A student says "the lamp uses up the current, so less comes out the other side." Explain clearly why this is wrong. If current isn't used up in a lamp, why does the battery eventually go flat?

### Section 3: Current Splits at Junctions

**Core idea:** When the path splits into branches, electrons split too. Some go one way, some go the other. The total is always conserved.

**Content:**
- So far we've seen one path (series). What happens when the wire splits into two branches?
- At a **junction** (where the wire splits), the electrons divide. Some take one branch, some take the other.
- **The total current entering the junction = the total current leaving the junction.** No electrons appear or disappear.
- Example: Battery pushes 0.8 A of current. The wire splits into two branches. Branch 1 carries 0.4 A, Branch 2 carries 0.4 A. Total: 0.4 + 0.4 = 0.8 A. Conserved.
- The branches don't have to be equal. If one branch is "easier" for electrons to flow through, more electrons take that path.
- Example: Battery pushes 1.2 A. Branch 1 carries 0.8 A, Branch 2 carries 0.4 A. Total: 0.8 + 0.4 = 1.2 A. Still conserved.
- When the branches rejoin, the currents add back up.

**Multiple angles:**
- Angle 1 (conservation): Think of 100 electrons arriving at a junction. They must all go somewhere — 60 might go left, 40 might go right. But all 100 are accounted for.
- Angle 2 (measurement): Place ammeters in each branch and near the battery. The branch readings always add up to the battery reading. Always.
- Angle 3 (adding branches): Add a third branch. Now the current splits three ways. But the total from the battery increases — the battery pushes harder because there are more paths available. (Why? We'll understand this fully when we learn about resistance.)
- Angle 4 (real-world observation): In your house, turning on an extra appliance doesn't reduce current to other appliances. It adds a new path, and the total current from the mains increases.

**Key insight:** Current is conserved at every junction. Total in = Total out. When paths split, current splits. When paths rejoin, currents add back up.

**Questions:**
- MCQs: If I_battery = 1.5 A and I_branch1 = 0.9 A, what is I_branch2? At a junction, does current increase/decrease/stay conserved? Adding a third branch — what happens to total current?
- Subjective: A circuit has a battery and three parallel branches carrying 0.3 A, 0.5 A, and 0.2 A. What is the current from the battery? Two branches carry different currents — what might explain why one carries more than the other? (Teaser for resistance.)

### Section 4: Voltage — Energy Given to Each Charge

**Core idea:** Voltage is the energy the battery gives to each unit of charge. It is work done per charge. This is real physics, connected to what you already know about work and energy.

**Content:**

**Building from what they know:**
- You know that **Work = Force x Distance** (W = F x d). A force moves an object through a distance, and work is done.
- In electricity, there's a parallel: **Work = Charge x Voltage** (W = Q x V). The battery moves charge through a voltage, and work is done.
- So voltage is the **work done per unit charge**. A 6V battery does 6 joules of work on every coulomb of charge that passes through it.
- This is why voltage is sometimes called **electromotive force (EMF)** — it's the "force" (in energy terms) that drives charge around the circuit.

**What voltage actually is:**
- Voltage is measured in **volts (V)** using a **voltmeter**.
- A voltmeter is connected **across** a component (in parallel with it) — it measures the energy difference between two points.
- The battery **gives** energy to charges (this is the battery's voltage / EMF).
- Components **take** energy from charges (this is the voltage drop across the component).
- Voltage exists across the battery even when the switch is open — the energy difference is there, ready. No current flows because the loop isn't complete, but the voltage is present.

**The key principle — voltage drops:**
- When charge flows through a component (like a resistor or lamp), it loses some of its energy.
- This loss of energy is what we call a **voltage drop** across that component.
- The component converts this electrical energy into other forms (light, heat, sound).
- The total energy given by the battery = the total energy dropped across all components. Energy is conserved.

**Multiple angles:**
- Angle 1 (energy bookkeeping): Battery gives 6 J per coulomb. If there's one lamp, it drops all 6 V. If there are two identical lamps in series, each drops 3 V. The total: 3 + 3 = 6 V. Energy in = energy out.
- Angle 2 (measurement): Connect a voltmeter across the battery — reads 6 V. Connect voltmeters across each lamp in series — the readings add up to 6 V. Always.
- Angle 3 (open circuit): Disconnect everything. Voltmeter across battery still reads 6 V. The energy difference exists whether or not charge is flowing. Voltage is a property of the battery, not of the current.
- Angle 4 (connection to force): Just as a force can exist without movement (you can push a wall — force exists, but no work is done because nothing moves), voltage can exist without current. Voltage is the potential to do work. Current is what happens when charges actually move.

**Key insight:** Voltage is energy per charge. The battery creates it. Components consume it (voltage drop). Total voltage given = total voltage dropped. Voltage exists even without current — it's the cause, current is the effect.

**Questions:**
- MCQs: What does a voltmeter measure? Where is a voltmeter connected? If a battery is 9V and one lamp drops 4V, what does the second lamp drop?
- Subjective: Explain in your own words what "6V" means in terms of energy and charge. A battery is connected to nothing (open circuit). Is there still a voltage? Explain why W = QV makes voltage similar to force in W = Fd.

### Section 5: Voltage in Series — The Energy Gets Shared

**Core idea:** In a series circuit, the battery's voltage is shared among all the components. Each component drops some voltage. The total of all drops equals the battery's voltage.

**Content:**
- In series, there is only one path. All charge flows through every component, one after another.
- The battery gives a fixed amount of energy per charge (its voltage). As charge passes through each component, it loses some energy. Each component takes its share.
- **V_battery = V_component1 + V_component2 + ...**
- This is energy conservation: the energy the battery gives must equal the total energy the components take.

**How voltage splits:**
- If all components are identical, voltage splits equally. (Two identical lamps on a 6V battery → 3V each.)
- If components are different, they don't get equal shares. The component that opposes the current more (higher resistance — we'll define this precisely in Section 7) gets a bigger share of the voltage. (We'll see why with the formula V = IR.)
- The battery doesn't "choose" how to split the voltage. The split is determined by the components themselves.

**What this means in practice:**
- More components in series → each one gets a smaller share of voltage → less energy per component → bulbs are dimmer.
- The battery's voltage doesn't change when you add components — but each component gets less of it.

**Multiple angles:**
- Angle 1 (numbers): 12V battery, three identical lamps. Each gets 4V. Add a fourth: each gets 3V. The battery is the same — the sharing changes.
- Angle 2 (unequal components): 6V battery, two components. Voltmeter reads 4V across one and 2V across the other. 4 + 2 = 6. The one with 4V is "using" more energy per charge.
- Angle 3 (single component): One lamp on a 6V battery. The lamp drops the full 6V. All the battery's energy goes to that one lamp.
- Angle 4 (why bulbs dim): Each bulb in series gets a fraction of the total voltage. Less voltage → less energy delivered → dimmer light. This is a real, observable consequence.

**Key insight:** Series means sharing. The battery's total voltage is divided among the components. More components = smaller share each = less energy each.

**Questions:**
- MCQs: Three lamps on 9V battery (equal) — voltage across each? If V1 = 5V on a 12V battery with two lamps, what is V2? Adding a lamp to a series circuit — what happens to voltage across each existing lamp?
- Subjective: A series circuit has a 6V battery and two lamps. Voltmeter across lamp 1 reads 2V, across lamp 2 reads 4V. Which lamp is "using" more energy per charge? Why? Explain why total voltage drops must equal battery voltage (hint: think about energy conservation).

### Section 6: Voltage in Parallel — Every Branch Gets the Full Amount

**Core idea:** In a parallel circuit, each branch connects directly to the battery. Every branch gets the full battery voltage. No sharing.

**Content:**
- In parallel, each branch is its own separate path from one terminal of the battery to the other.
- Because each branch connects directly to both terminals, the voltage across each branch equals the battery's voltage.
- **V_battery = V_branch1 = V_branch2 = V_branch3 = ...**
- This is fundamentally different from series. In series, components share voltage. In parallel, every branch gets the full voltage.

**Why?**
- Trace any single branch: it starts at the + terminal and ends at the - terminal. The energy difference between those two points IS the battery's voltage. Every branch experiences this same energy difference.
- Adding more branches doesn't reduce the voltage available to any branch. Each branch is independent.

**What this means in practice:**
- Every lamp in parallel gets the same voltage → same energy per charge → same brightness.
- Adding more lamps in parallel doesn't dim the existing ones.
- BUT: each branch draws its own current. More branches = more total current from the battery = battery drains faster.

**Voltage drops within a parallel branch:**
- If a single branch has multiple components in series (within that branch), those components share the branch voltage among themselves — just like a series circuit.
- But the total voltage across the branch is still equal to the battery voltage.

**Multiple angles:**
- Angle 1 (measurement): Connect voltmeters across each branch. All read the same as the battery voltage. Always.
- Angle 2 (adding branches): Start with one lamp (6V across it). Add a second lamp in parallel. Voltmeter across the first lamp: still 6V. The first lamp doesn't "know" or "care" that another branch was added.
- Angle 3 (independence): Remove one branch (or a lamp breaks in one branch). Other branches are unaffected. Their voltage doesn't change. Their current doesn't change. They are independent paths.
- Angle 4 (real world): Your house is wired in parallel. The TV gets 230V. The fridge gets 230V. Turning on the TV doesn't affect the fridge's voltage.

**Key insight:** Parallel means independence. Each branch gets the full battery voltage because each connects directly to the battery. Adding or removing branches doesn't affect the others.

**Questions:**
- MCQs: Three branches on a 6V battery — voltage across each? One branch is removed — what happens to voltage across the others? How is voltmeter connected to measure voltage across a parallel branch?
- Subjective: Explain why every branch in a parallel circuit gets the same voltage (hint: trace the path). If all branches get the same voltage, why does the battery drain faster with more branches?

### Section 7: Resistance — What Causes Voltage to Drop

**Core idea:** A resistor opposes the flow of current. When current flows through a resistor, voltage drops across it. Resistance is what connects voltage and current together.

**Content:**

**What is resistance?**
- Every component offers some opposition to the flow of charge. This opposition is called **resistance**.
- Measured in **ohms** (symbol: omega).
- Inside a conductor, electrons collide with atoms as they move through. These collisions slow the electrons down and transfer energy to the atoms (which is why components get warm/hot).
- More collisions = more opposition = higher resistance.

**The relationship — Ohm's Law:**
- We've established: voltage is the energy per charge (the push), current is the flow of charge (the result).
- Resistance is what sits between them: **R = V / I**
- Rearranged: **I = V / R** — if you increase voltage, current increases. If you increase resistance, current decreases.
- Rearranged: **V = I x R** — the voltage dropped across a component equals the current through it times its resistance.
- This isn't just a formula to memorize. It's the fundamental relationship: **voltage drives, resistance opposes, current is what results.**

**V = IR explains everything we've seen:**
- Series circuits: current is the same through all components (I is constant). So V = IR tells us that the component with bigger R gets a bigger voltage drop. That's WHY voltage splits unevenly between different components.
- Parallel circuits: voltage is the same across all branches (V is constant). So I = V/R tells us that the branch with smaller R gets more current. That's WHY current splits unevenly between different branches.

**Resistance in series:**
- Total resistance = R1 + R2 + R3 + ...
- More components → more total opposition → less current from the same battery.

**Resistance in parallel:**
- Adding more branches **decreases** total resistance.
- Why? Each new branch is another path for charge to flow. More paths = less overall opposition.
- This is why total current from the battery increases when you add parallel branches.

**Types of resistors:**
- **Fixed resistor** — constant resistance. Circuit symbol: rectangle / zigzag.
- **Variable resistor** — adjustable. Used in dimmer switches, volume controls. Circuit symbol: resistor with an arrow.

**Multiple angles:**
- Angle 1 (V = IR in series): 6V battery, two resistors: 10 ohm and 20 ohm. Total R = 30 ohm. Current I = 6/30 = 0.2 A. Voltage across 10 ohm: 0.2 x 10 = 2V. Voltage across 20 ohm: 0.2 x 20 = 4V. Total: 2 + 4 = 6V. Everything checks out.
- Angle 2 (V = IR in parallel): 6V battery, two branches: 12 ohm and 6 ohm. Branch 1: I = 6/12 = 0.5 A. Branch 2: I = 6/6 = 1.0 A. Total current: 1.5 A. Lower resistance branch carries more current.
- Angle 3 (why resistance causes voltage drop): A resistor opposes charge flow. To push charge through it, energy must be spent. That energy expenditure IS the voltage drop. Higher resistance = more energy needed = bigger voltage drop.
- Angle 4 (practical): A variable resistor lets you control how much current flows. Turn up resistance → less current → dimmer lamp. Turn down resistance → more current → brighter lamp.

**Key insight:** Resistance is the bridge between voltage and current. V = IR is the single most important equation in basic circuits. It explains why voltage splits in series and why current splits in parallel.

**Questions:**
- MCQs: Calculate R given V and I. If resistance doubles and voltage stays the same, what happens to current? In series, which resistor has the biggest voltage drop?
- Subjective: A 6V battery is connected to a 12 ohm resistor. Calculate the current. Now a second 12 ohm resistor is added in series. Calculate the new current and voltage across each resistor. Explain in your own words why adding a parallel branch reduces total resistance.

### Section 8: Putting It All Together — Series vs Parallel

**Core idea:** Consolidate all the rules. See them side by side. Apply them to mixed problems.

**Content:**

**The complete picture:**

| Property | Series | Parallel |
|----------|--------|----------|
| Current | Same through all components | Splits at junctions (adds up to battery current) |
| Voltage | Splits across components (adds up to battery voltage) | Same across all branches (equals battery voltage) |
| Resistance | R_total = R1 + R2 + ... (increases) | R_total decreases with more branches |
| One component breaks | Whole circuit stops (loop broken) | Other branches unaffected |
| Add more components | Current decreases, each gets less voltage | Total current increases, each gets full voltage |

**The three fundamental rules:**
1. **Charge is conserved.** Current in = current out, at every point, always.
2. **Energy is conserved.** Total voltage given by battery = total voltage dropped across components.
3. **V = IR.** Voltage, current, and resistance are always linked by this relationship.

Everything about series and parallel circuits follows from these three rules. There is nothing else to memorize — just apply these three.

**Identifying series and parallel:**
- Series: components are on the SAME single path. Current must flow through all of them, one after another.
- Parallel: components are on DIFFERENT paths (branches). Current splits and each branch is independent.
- Circuit diagrams showing both, with clear identification.

**Questions:**
- MCQs: Mixed series/parallel identification, calculation problems, predict what happens when a change is made
- Subjective: A student has a 12V battery, a 6 ohm resistor and a 3 ohm resistor. Compare what happens when they are connected in series vs in parallel (calculate current, voltage across each). Why are houses wired in parallel and not in series? Given a circuit diagram, identify which parts are in series and which are in parallel.

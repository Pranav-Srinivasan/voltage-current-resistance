1. The Electric Circuit — A Complete Loop

For electricity to work, charges need a complete path.

Concepts

What are electric charges?

Everything around you is made of atoms. Inside every atom, there are tiny particles called electrons that carry a negative electric charge.

In metals (like copper wire), some electrons are free to move. These free electrons are what carry electric current through a circuit.

A battery has two terminals: positive (+) and negative (-). Inside the battery, a chemical reaction creates an energy difference between these two terminals. This energy difference is what pushes electrons through the circuit.

The complete loop

For electrons to flow, they need a complete, unbroken path — from one terminal of the battery, through the wires and components, and back to the other terminal.

This complete path is called a circuit.

  • If the path is complete (closed circuit) — electrons flow.
  • If the path is broken anywhere (open circuit) — electrons cannot flow. But the battery's energy difference is still there, ready and waiting.

Components in a circuit

  • Battery / Cell — provides energy to push charges around the circuit.
  • Wires — the paths that charges travel through.
  • Lamps / Buzzers — components that convert electrical energy into light, sound, or heat.
  • Switches — break or complete the circuit. Open switch = broken loop = no current.
  • Resistors — oppose the flow of charge (more on this later).

Two types of circuits

Series circuit: A single loop. All components are connected one after another on the same path. There is only one route for charge to take.

Parallel circuit: The path splits into two or more branches. Each branch is a separate route for charge. The branches reconnect before returning to the battery.

We will study each type in detail in later sections. For now, just know these two arrangements exist.

Angle 1 — Trace the path

Draw a simple circuit: battery → wire → lamp → wire → back to battery. Can you trace a complete loop from one terminal to the other? Yes. Electrons flow. The lamp lights.

Angle 2 — Open the switch

Now open the switch. The loop is broken. No electrons can flow — the current is zero everywhere. But the battery hasn't changed. Its energy difference is still there. The moment you close the switch, current flows instantly.

Angle 3 — A broken bulb

What if the filament inside a lamp snaps? The loop is broken at the lamp. It's the same as opening a switch — no current flows anywhere in the circuit. Every component stops working, not just the broken one.

Key Insight

The battery provides energy. The complete loop lets electrons carry that energy to the components. Break the loop anywhere, and everything stops. But the battery's energy is still there — waiting for the loop to be restored.

Think Deeper

Q4: A student connects a battery to a lamp but forgets one wire. Will the lamp light? Explain why or why not.

No, the lamp will not light. Without both wires, there is no complete loop. Electrons need a continuous path from one terminal of the battery, through the lamp, and back to the other terminal. A missing wire breaks this loop, so no current can flow.

Q5: When a switch is open, is the battery "doing nothing"? Explain.

The battery still has its energy difference (voltage) between its terminals — the chemical reaction inside hasn't stopped. It's ready to push charges the moment the circuit is complete. So the battery isn't "doing nothing" — it's maintaining an energy difference. But no current flows because there's no complete path.

Q6: In your own words, explain why a circuit must be a complete loop for current to flow.

Electrons need somewhere to go AND somewhere to come back to. If the path is broken at any point, electrons at that break have nowhere to move — and since they can't jump across a gap, the entire flow stops. It doesn't matter where the break is — a break anywhere stops the flow everywhere, because every electron in the loop depends on the one ahead of it being able to move forward.

2. Current — The Flow of Charge

Current is how much charge flows past a point each second. It is never used up.

Concepts

What is electric current?

When a circuit is complete, electrons move through the wires and components. This movement of charge is called electric current.

Current tells you: how much charge is passing a point every second.

It is measured in amperes (A) using an instrument called an ammeter.

An ammeter must be placed in the path of the current (in series with the component you're measuring). The charge must flow through the ammeter so it can count how much is passing.

The most important rule of current

Current is NEVER used up.

Place an ammeter before a lamp. It reads 0.5 A.

Place another ammeter after the same lamp. It also reads 0.5 A.

The same amount of charge that enters the lamp also exits the lamp. No charge is created inside the lamp. No charge is destroyed inside the lamp.

The lamp glows not because it "consumes" electrons, but because electrons transfer their energy to the lamp as they pass through. The electrons themselves keep moving. They come out the other side with less energy — but the same number of electrons come out.

In a series circuit: current is the same everywhere

Since there is only one path, every electron must pass through every component. No electrons leave the path. No new electrons join.

So the current reading is identical at every point in a series circuit — before the first lamp, between the lamps, after the last lamp, at the battery. All the same.

Angle 1 — Measurement

Put ammeters at three different points in a series circuit. All three read the same value. This isn't theory — it's what you actually observe. You can verify it yourself with real ammeters.

Angle 2 — Conservation

Charge is conserved. This is a fundamental law of physics. Electrons don't vanish inside a lamp and don't appear out of nothing. What goes in must come out. If 100 electrons enter a lamp per second, 100 electrons exit per second.

Angle 3 — Energy vs Charge

The battery gives energy to the electrons. The lamp takes energy from the electrons. But the electrons themselves keep circulating. When the battery eventually "dies," it has run out of energy to give — it has NOT run out of electrons to push.

Common Mistake

"The first lamp gets more current than the second."

No. Measure it. The current is exactly the same before the first lamp, between the lamps, and after the second lamp. Electrons don't "slow down" or "get used up" as they pass through components. They lose energy, not quantity.

Key Insight

Current is conserved. Always. In any single path, the current entering equals the current leaving. Electrons carry energy — they deliver it at components — but the electrons themselves keep flowing around the loop.

Think Deeper

Q4: A student says: "The lamp uses up the current, so less comes out the other side." Explain clearly why this is wrong.

The lamp does not "use up" current. The same number of electrons that enter the lamp also exit it — charge is conserved. What the lamp does is take energy from the electrons. The electrons enter with high energy (given by the battery) and exit with less energy (which has been converted to light and heat). But the flow of electrons — the current — is unchanged.

Q5: If current isn't used up in a lamp, why does a battery eventually go flat?

The battery runs out of energy, not electrons. The chemical reaction inside the battery is what provides energy to push the electrons. When the chemicals are used up, the battery can no longer give energy to the electrons — so even though the electrons are still there, nothing is pushing them. The battery goes flat because its energy source is exhausted, not because it ran out of charge to push.

Q6: A series circuit has a battery, a lamp, and a buzzer. The ammeter near the battery reads 0.4 A. What is the current between the lamp and the buzzer? What is the current after the buzzer?

Both are 0.4 A. In a series circuit, there is only one path. The current is the same at every point — near the battery, between the lamp and buzzer, and after the buzzer. Current is never used up.

3. Current Splits at Junctions

When the path divides, electrons divide too. The total is always conserved.

Concepts

What happens at a junction?

So far we've looked at circuits with a single path. But what happens when the wire splits into two or more branches?

At a junction (where the wire splits), the electrons divide. Some take one branch, some take the other.

The fundamental rule still holds: charge is conserved.

Itotal = Ibranch 1 + Ibranch 2 + ...
The total current entering a junction equals the total current leaving it.

Equal and unequal splits

Equal split: Battery pushes 0.8 A. Wire splits into two identical branches. Each branch carries 0.4 A. Total: 0.4 + 0.4 = 0.8 A. Conserved.

Unequal split: Battery pushes 1.2 A. Branch 1 carries 0.8 A, Branch 2 carries 0.4 A. Total: 0.8 + 0.4 = 1.2 A. Still conserved.

The split doesn't have to be equal. If one branch is "easier" for electrons to flow through (less resistance — we'll quantify this later), more electrons take that path.

When branches rejoin

After the branches, the wires come back together before returning to the battery. At this point, the currents from all branches add back up to give the total current.

What splits must recombine. No electrons are lost along the way.

Angle 1 — Counting electrons

Imagine 100 electrons arriving at a junction every second. They must all go somewhere. Maybe 60 go left and 40 go right. But all 100 are accounted for. You can't have 60 going left and 60 going right — that would be 120 electrons from 100. Impossible.

Angle 2 — Measurement

Place an ammeter in each branch and one near the battery. The branch readings always add up to the battery reading. This is not approximate — it is exact. You can verify this in any lab.

Angle 3 — Adding more branches

Add a third branch. Now the current splits three ways. But something interesting happens: the total current from the battery increases. Why? Because there are more paths available for electrons. The battery responds by pushing more total current. (We'll understand exactly why when we learn about resistance.)

Angle 4 — Your house

In your house, turning on an extra appliance doesn't reduce the current to other appliances. It creates a new path, and the total current from the mains supply increases. Each appliance operates independently on its own branch.

Key Insight

Current is conserved at every junction. Total in = total out. When paths split, current splits. When paths rejoin, currents add back up. The total number of electrons is always accounted for.

Q5: Two branches in a parallel circuit carry different currents — one carries 0.8 A and the other carries 0.3 A. What might explain why one carries more than the other?

The branch carrying more current (0.8 A) has less opposition (resistance) to the flow of charge. Electrons take the "easier" path in greater numbers. The branch carrying 0.3 A has more resistance — something in that branch makes it harder for electrons to flow through. We will study resistance in detail in Section 7.

Q6: A student says: "If I add more branches, the current in each branch gets smaller because the battery's current is shared." Is this correct? Explain.

This is not correct. When you add a new branch, the current in the existing branches doesn't change — each branch is independent. What happens is the battery pushes more total current because there are more paths available. The new branch gets its own current on top of what the other branches already carry. The battery works harder, not the same amount spread thinner.

4. Voltage — Energy Given to Each Charge

Voltage is the work done per unit charge. It is the push. It exists even without current.

Concepts

Building from what you know

You already know from mechanics that Work = Force × Distance (W = F × d). A force moves an object through a distance, and work is done — energy is transferred.

In electricity, there is a direct parallel:

Work = Charge × Voltage
W = Q × V

The battery moves charge through a voltage, and work is done — energy is transferred to the charges.

So voltage is the work done per unit charge. A 6V battery does 6 joules of work on every coulomb of charge that passes through it.

This is why voltage is sometimes called electromotive force (EMF) — it's the "force" (in energy terms) that drives charge around the circuit.

What voltage actually is

Voltage is measured in volts (V) using a voltmeter.

A voltmeter is connected across a component (in parallel with it). It measures the energy difference between two points.

  • The battery gives energy to charges — this is the battery's voltage (EMF).
  • Components take energy from charges — this is called the voltage drop across the component.

Voltage is also called potential difference (p.d.) — the difference in energy per charge between two points.

The key principle — voltage drops

When charge flows through a component (like a resistor or lamp), it loses some of its energy. This loss is the voltage drop.

The component converts this electrical energy into other forms — light, heat, or sound.

The total energy given by the battery = the total energy dropped across all components. Energy is conserved.

Angle 1 — Energy bookkeeping

Battery gives 6 J per coulomb. If there's one lamp, it drops all 6 V. If there are two identical lamps in series, each drops 3 V. Total: 3 + 3 = 6 V. Energy given = energy taken. Always.

Angle 2 — Measurement

Connect a voltmeter across the battery — reads 6 V. Connect voltmeters across each component in series — the readings add up to 6 V. This is a measurable, verifiable fact.

Angle 3 — Open circuit

Disconnect everything. Voltmeter across the battery still reads 6 V. The energy difference exists whether or not charge is flowing. Voltage is a property of the battery, not a result of current.

Angle 4 — Voltage without current

Just as a force can exist without movement (you can push against a wall — force is there, but nothing moves and no work is done), voltage can exist without current. Voltage is the potential to do work. Current is what happens when charges actually move through a complete circuit.

Key Insight

Voltage is the cause. Current is the effect. The battery creates voltage (energy per charge). Components consume it (voltage drop). Total voltage given = total voltage dropped. Voltage exists even without current — it's the push that's always there.

Q4: Explain in your own words what "6 V" means in terms of energy and charge.

Q5: We know Work = Force × Distance in mechanics. In electricity, Work = Charge × Voltage. Explain what makes voltage similar to force in this comparison.

In mechanics, force is what drives an object through a distance to do work. In electricity, voltage is what drives charge through the circuit to do work. Both are the "driving agent" — the thing that makes energy transfer happen. Just as increasing force (with the same distance) increases work done, increasing voltage (with the same charge) increases the work done on the charges. This is why voltage is called electromotive force — it's the electrical equivalent of a driving force.

Q6: A student says: "If no current flows, there can't be any voltage." Is this correct? Explain with an example.

This is incorrect. A battery with an open switch has voltage across its terminals even though no current flows. The chemical reaction inside the battery maintains the energy difference (voltage) between the terminals regardless of whether there's a complete circuit. Voltage is the cause — it's always there. Current is the effect — it only flows when there's a complete path. You can have voltage without current, but you cannot have current without voltage.

5. Voltage in Series — The Energy Gets Shared

In a series circuit, the battery's voltage is divided among the components. Each one takes its share.

Concepts

How voltage behaves in series

In a series circuit, there is only one path. All charge flows through every component, one after another.

The battery gives a fixed amount of energy per charge (its voltage). As charge passes through each component, it loses some energy. Each component takes its share.

Vbattery = Vcomponent 1 + Vcomponent 2 + ...
The voltage drops across all components add up to the battery's voltage.

This is energy conservation: the energy the battery gives must equal the total energy the components take.

How the voltage splits

If all components are identical, voltage splits equally. Two identical lamps on a 6 V battery → 3 V each.

If components are different, they don't get equal shares. The component that opposes current more (higher resistance) gets a bigger share of the voltage. We'll see exactly why with the formula V = IR in Section 7.

The battery doesn't "choose" how to split the voltage. The split is determined by the components themselves.

What this means in practice

  • More components in series → each one gets a smaller share of the battery's voltage.
  • Less voltage per component → less energy delivered → bulbs are dimmer.
  • The battery's voltage doesn't change when you add components — but each component's share gets smaller.
Angle 1 — Numbers

12 V battery, three identical lamps. Each gets 4 V. Now add a fourth lamp: each gets 3 V. The battery is the same — the sharing changes. More lamps = smaller share each.

Angle 2 — Unequal components

6 V battery, two components. Voltmeter reads 4 V across one and 2 V across the other. 4 + 2 = 6. The one with 4 V is "using" more energy per charge — it has higher resistance.

Angle 3 — Single component

One lamp on a 6 V battery. The lamp drops the full 6 V. All the battery's energy goes to that one component. There's nothing else to share with.

Angle 4 — Why bulbs dim

Each bulb in series gets a fraction of the total voltage. Less voltage means less energy delivered to each bulb. Less energy means less light. This is observable — add a second bulb in series and watch both bulbs become noticeably dimmer than one bulb alone.

Key Insight

Series means sharing. The battery's total voltage is divided among the components. More components = smaller share each = less energy delivered to each one. The total of all voltage drops always equals the battery voltage.

Q4: A series circuit has a 6 V battery and two lamps. Voltmeter across lamp 1 reads 2 V, across lamp 2 reads 4 V. Which lamp is "using" more energy per charge? Why might they be different?

Lamp 2 is using more energy per charge (4 V vs 2 V). They are different because the lamps are not identical — lamp 2 has higher resistance, so it opposes the current more and therefore takes a larger share of the battery's energy. The total (2 + 4 = 6 V) still equals the battery voltage.

Q5: Explain why the total voltage drops must equal the battery voltage. (Hint: think about energy conservation.)

The battery gives a fixed amount of energy to each coulomb of charge (that's what its voltage means). As the charge travels through the circuit, each component takes some of that energy. By the time the charge returns to the battery, all the energy must have been transferred to the components — none can be "left over" and none can appear from nowhere. So the total energy taken by all components (sum of voltage drops) must exactly equal the energy given by the battery. This is the law of conservation of energy applied to circuits.

Q6: Three identical resistors are connected in series to a 15 V battery. What is the voltage across each? Now one resistor is replaced with one that has double the resistance. Without calculating, will it get more or less than 5 V? Why?

With three identical resistors: 15 V / 3 = 5 V each.

When one is replaced with a higher resistance: it will get more than 5 V. A component with higher resistance opposes current more, so it "uses" more energy per charge, which means a larger voltage drop. The other two (smaller) resistors will now each get less than 5 V, but the total will still be 15 V.

6. Voltage in Parallel — Every Branch Gets the Full Amount

In a parallel circuit, each branch connects directly to the battery. No sharing.

Concepts

How voltage behaves in parallel

In a parallel circuit, each branch is its own separate path from one terminal of the battery to the other.

Because each branch connects directly to both terminals of the battery, the voltage across each branch equals the battery's voltage.

Vbattery = Vbranch 1 = Vbranch 2 = Vbranch 3 = ...
Every branch gets the full battery voltage.

This is fundamentally different from series. In series, components share voltage. In parallel, every branch gets the full amount.

Why does every branch get the full voltage?

Trace any single branch: it starts at the + terminal and ends at the − terminal. The energy difference between those two points IS the battery's voltage.

Every branch experiences this same energy difference, because every branch connects to the same two points (the battery's terminals).

Adding more branches doesn't reduce the voltage available to any branch. Each branch is independent.

What this means in practice

  • Every lamp in parallel gets the same voltage → same energy per charge → same brightness.
  • Adding more lamps in parallel doesn't dim the existing ones.
  • But: each branch draws its own current. More branches = more total current from the battery = the battery drains faster.
Angle 1 — Measurement

Connect voltmeters across each branch of a parallel circuit. They all read the same value — the battery's voltage. Always. No exceptions.

Angle 2 — Adding branches

Start with one lamp on a 6 V battery: 6 V across it. Add a second lamp in parallel. Voltmeter across the first lamp: still 6 V. The first lamp doesn't "know" or "care" that another branch was added. It still connects to the same battery terminals.

Angle 3 — Independence

Remove one branch (or a lamp breaks). Other branches are unaffected. Their voltage doesn't change. Their current doesn't change. They are independent paths, each connected to the battery on their own.

Angle 4 — Your house

Your house is wired in parallel. The TV gets 230 V. The fridge gets 230 V. The lamp gets 230 V. Turning on the TV doesn't change the fridge's voltage. Each appliance connects independently to the mains supply.

Key Insight

Parallel means independence. Each branch gets the full battery voltage because each connects directly to the battery terminals. Adding or removing branches doesn't affect the voltage across the others.

Q4: Explain why every branch in a parallel circuit gets the same voltage. Use the idea of "tracing the path" in your answer.

Every branch starts at the positive terminal and ends at the negative terminal of the battery. The voltage is the energy difference between these two terminals. Since every branch connects to the same two terminals, every branch experiences the same energy difference. It doesn't matter how many branches there are — each one "sees" the same starting and ending point.

Q5: If all branches in a parallel circuit get the same voltage, why does the battery drain faster when you add more branches?

Each branch draws its own current independently. When you add a new branch, the existing branches still draw the same current as before, but the new branch draws additional current. The total current from the battery increases. More current means the battery is delivering more energy per second, so its stored energy is used up faster. The voltage per branch stays the same, but the total current (and therefore total energy delivery) goes up.

Q6: Compare series and parallel: In series, two identical lamps on a 6 V battery each get ___ V. In parallel, two identical lamps on a 6 V battery each get ___ V. Which arrangement gives brighter lamps? Why?

In series: each lamp gets 3 V (voltage is shared: 6/2 = 3). In parallel: each lamp gets 6 V (each branch gets the full voltage). The parallel arrangement gives brighter lamps because each lamp receives more energy per charge (6 V vs 3 V), so more energy is converted to light.

7. Resistance — What Causes Voltage to Drop

Resistance opposes current. It connects voltage and current through one simple equation.

Concepts

What is resistance?

Every component offers some opposition to the flow of charge. This opposition is called resistance.

It is measured in ohms (symbol: Ω).

Inside a conductor, electrons collide with atoms as they move through. These collisions slow the electrons down and transfer energy from the electrons to the atoms. This is why components with high resistance get hot — the energy of the moving electrons is being converted to heat.

The relationship — Ohm's Law

We've established that voltage is the push (energy per charge) and current is the flow. Resistance sits between them:

R = V / I
Resistance = Voltage / Current

Rearranged:

I = V / R
More voltage → more current. More resistance → less current.
V = I × R
The voltage drop across a component = current through it × its resistance.

This is not just a formula to memorize. It captures the fundamental relationship: voltage drives, resistance opposes, current is what results.

V = IR explains everything we've seen

Series circuits: Current is the same through all components (I is constant). So V = IR tells us that the component with bigger R gets a bigger voltage drop. That's why voltage splits unevenly between different components in series.

Parallel circuits: Voltage is the same across all branches (V is constant). So I = V/R tells us that the branch with smaller R gets more current. That's why current splits unevenly between different branches in parallel.

Resistance in series and parallel

Series: Total resistance = R1 + R2 + R3 + ...

More components in series means more total opposition. More resistance means less current from the same battery.

Parallel: Adding more branches decreases total resistance.

1/Rtotal = 1/R1 + 1/R2 + ...
The reciprocal of total resistance = sum of reciprocals of each branch.

Why? Each new branch is another path for charge to flow. More paths = less overall opposition. This is why total current from the battery increases when you add parallel branches.

Types of resistors

Fixed resistor — its resistance stays constant. Used when you want a specific, unchanging resistance in a circuit.

Variable resistor — its resistance can be adjusted. Used in dimmer switches (control brightness) and volume controls (control current to speakers).

Angle 1 — V = IR in series (worked example)

6 V battery, two resistors: 10 Ω and 20 Ω in series.

Total R = 10 + 20 = 30 Ω. Current I = V/R = 6/30 = 0.2 A.

Voltage across 10 Ω: V = 0.2 × 10 = 2 V.

Voltage across 20 Ω: V = 0.2 × 20 = 4 V.

Check: 2 + 4 = 6 V. Matches the battery. Everything works.

Angle 2 — V = IR in parallel (worked example)

6 V battery, two branches: 12 Ω and 6 Ω in parallel.

Branch 1: I = V/R = 6/12 = 0.5 A.

Branch 2: I = V/R = 6/6 = 1.0 A.

Total current = 0.5 + 1.0 = 1.5 A. The lower-resistance branch carries more current.

Angle 3 — Why resistance causes voltage drop

A resistor opposes charge flow. To push charge through it, energy must be spent. That energy expenditure IS the voltage drop. Higher resistance = more energy needed per charge to push through = bigger voltage drop.

Angle 4 — Practical application

A variable resistor lets you control current. Turn up resistance → less current → dimmer lamp. Turn down resistance → more current → brighter lamp. The voltage from the battery hasn't changed — you've changed how much the circuit resists it.

Key Insight

V = IR is the single most important equation in basic circuits. It bridges voltage, current, and resistance. It explains why voltage splits in series (same I, different R → different V). It explains why current splits in parallel (same V, different R → different I). Master this equation and its meaning, and circuits become straightforward.

Q4: A 6 V battery is connected to a 12 Ω resistor. Calculate the current. Now a second 12 Ω resistor is added in series. Calculate the new total resistance, the new current, and the voltage across each resistor.

Single resistor: I = V/R = 6/12 = 0.5 A.

Two in series: Total R = 12 + 12 = 24 Ω. New current I = 6/24 = 0.25 A. Voltage across each: V = IR = 0.25 × 12 = 3 V. Check: 3 + 3 = 6 V.

Adding the second resistor doubled the resistance and halved the current. Each resistor now gets half the battery voltage.

Q5: A buzzer has more resistance than a lamp. Both are connected to the same battery in separate (parallel) branches. Which one carries more current? Explain using V = IR.

The lamp carries more current. In parallel, both get the same voltage (V). Using I = V/R, the component with less resistance gets more current. Since the lamp has less resistance than the buzzer, more current flows through the lamp.

Q6: Explain in your own words why adding a parallel branch reduces the total resistance of the circuit.

When you add a new branch in parallel, you're giving the electrons another path to flow through. The existing path is still there and unchanged, but now there's an additional route. More available paths means the overall opposition to current is lower — it's easier for charge to flow through the circuit as a whole. The result is that the total resistance decreases, and the total current from the battery increases.

Q7: Why does a bulb get hot when current flows through it? Connect your answer to the idea of resistance.

The filament of a bulb has high resistance. As electrons flow through it, they collide with the atoms in the filament. These collisions transfer energy from the electrons to the atoms, causing them to vibrate more — which we observe as heat and light. The higher the resistance, the more energy is transferred per charge (higher voltage drop), and the hotter the component gets.

8. Putting It All Together

Three rules govern all circuits. Everything else follows from them.

Summary

The three fundamental rules

  1. Charge is conserved. Current in = current out, at every point, always. Electrons are never created or destroyed in a circuit.
  2. Energy is conserved. Total voltage given by the battery = total voltage dropped across components. Energy in = energy out.
  3. V = IR. Voltage, current, and resistance are always linked by this relationship. It governs how energy and charge distribute themselves in any circuit.

Everything about series and parallel circuits follows from these three rules. There is nothing else to memorize — just apply them.

Identifying series and parallel

Series: Components are on the same single path. Current must flow through all of them, one after another. There is no alternative route.

Parallel: Components are on different paths (branches). Current splits, and each branch is independent. The branches start and end at the same two points.

A helpful test: if removing one component breaks the entire circuit, it's in series. If other components still work, it's in parallel.

How to approach any circuit problem

  1. Identify which parts are in series and which are in parallel.
  2. Apply the current rule: Same in series, splits in parallel.
  3. Apply the voltage rule: Splits in series, same in parallel.
  4. Use V = IR to calculate whatever you need.
  5. Check: Do the voltages add up to the battery voltage? Do the currents add up at junctions? If yes, your answer is likely correct.

Key equations

V = I × R
Voltage = Current × Resistance
I = V / R

R = V / I
Resistance = Voltage / Current
W = Q × V
Work done = Charge × Voltage
The Big Picture

The battery provides energy (voltage). The circuit determines how that energy is distributed. In series, components share the energy. In parallel, each branch gets the full energy independently. Resistance determines how much current flows and how the voltage distributes. V = IR ties it all together.

Q4: A student has a 12 V battery, a 6 Ω resistor and a 3 Ω resistor. Compare what happens when they are connected in series vs in parallel. Calculate current and voltage across each resistor for both cases.

Series: Rtotal = 6 + 3 = 9 Ω. I = 12/9 = 4/3 A ≈ 1.33 A (same through both). V across 6 Ω = (4/3) × 6 = 8 V. V across 3 Ω = (4/3) × 3 = 4 V. Check: 8 + 4 = 12 V.

Parallel: V across both = 12 V. I through 6 Ω = 12/6 = 2 A. I through 3 Ω = 12/3 = 4 A. Total current = 2 + 4 = 6 A.

Notice: parallel draws much more current (6 A vs 1.33 A) because the total resistance is lower and each branch gets the full voltage.

Q5: Why are houses wired in parallel and not in series? Give at least two reasons.

Reason 1 — Independence: In parallel, each appliance works independently. Turning off the TV doesn't affect the fridge. If one appliance breaks, others keep working. In series, one broken appliance would stop everything.

Reason 2 — Full voltage: In parallel, every appliance gets the full mains voltage (e.g., 230 V). In series, the voltage would be shared — each appliance would get only a fraction, and they wouldn't work properly. Appliances are designed to run at a specific voltage.

Q6: State the three fundamental rules that govern all circuits. Then explain how each one applies to a series circuit with a 6 V battery and two 10 Ω resistors.

Rule 1 — Charge conserved: The current is the same through both resistors: I = V/R = 6/20 = 0.3 A everywhere.

Rule 2 — Energy conserved: V across first resistor = 0.3 × 10 = 3 V. V across second = 0.3 × 10 = 3 V. Total: 3 + 3 = 6 V = battery voltage.

Rule 3 — V = IR: This was used to calculate both the current (I = V/Rtotal) and the individual voltage drops (V = IR). All three rules work together consistently.
